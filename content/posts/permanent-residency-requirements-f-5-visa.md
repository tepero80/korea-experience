## What is the F-5 Permanent Residency Visa?

The F-5 visa is the highest status a foreigner can hold in South Korea without becoming a naturalized citizen. It effectively treats you as a quasi-citizen in terms of employment and residence rights. Once obtained, you do not need to visit the immigration office for visa extensions. Instead, you only need to renew your physical alien registration card every 10 years, a simple administrative process that takes less than 30 minutes.

Learn more in our comprehensive guide to [Digital Nomad Visa Korea Requirements and Application](/blog/digital-nomad-visa-korea-requirements-and-application).

The most significant advantage is the decoupling of your visa from your employer. On an E-7 or E-2 visa, losing your job means you have 14 to 30 days to find new employment or leave the country. With an F-5, you can be unemployed, freelance, start a business, or take a sabbatical without risking your residency.

## Core Requirements: The "Big Three"

Regardless of which specific F-5 code you apply for, immigration officers will scrutinize three main pillars of your life in Korea. Failing to meet the threshold in any one of these areas results in immediate rejection.

### 1. Income Eligibility (The GNI Standard)
The financial requirement is the most common hurdle. For most F-5 categories, your annual income must meet or exceed the Gross National Income (GNI) per capita for the previous year. For 2026 applications, this is based on 2025 statistics.

Learn more in our comprehensive guide to [F 6 marriage visa requirements and interview comprehensiv...](/blog/f-6-marriage-visa-requirements-and-interview).

*   **F-5-1 (General):** Must earn **2x** the GNI.
*   **F-5-10 (Advanced Degree):** Must earn **1x** the GNI.
*   **F-5-16 (From F-2-7):** Must earn **1x** the GNI.

The GNI figure is updated annually by the Bank of Korea, usually in March or April. If you apply in January 2026, immigration may still use the 2024 GNI standard until the official 2025 numbers are released. It is crucial to check the specific amount required at the time of your application.

### 2. Korean Language Proficiency (KIIP)
Unless you are a high-value investor or hold a PhD in specific fields, you must prove your ability to integrate into Korean society. This is done through the **Korea Immigration and Integration Program (KIIP)**.

Learn more in our comprehensive guide to [F Visa Family Reunion Requirements Guide for](/blog/f-visa-family-reunion-requirements).

You generally need a **Level 5 Completion Certificate** (permanent residency track). This requires passing a comprehensive exam covering Korean language, history, culture, and politics. The Level 5 course is 70 hours long (60 hours basic + 10 hours specialized).

<InfoBox type="tip" title="KIIP Strategy">
  Classes fill up within 2 minutes of registration opening. Set an alarm for 8:59 AM on registration day. Alternatively, if you score 81+ points on the pre-test, you can skip straight to the Level 5 class without taking levels 1-4.
</InfoBox>

### 3. Conduct and Character
You must submit an overseas criminal record check from your home country (and any country where you resided for more than 1 year in the last 10 years). This document must be apostilled or consular verified.

Furthermore, your record in Korea must be clean. A fine of **â‚©3,000,000** or more for a violation of the Immigration Control Act, or a combined total of **â‚©5,000,000** in fines over the last 3 years, will disqualify you. Even minor traffic violations can add up, so maintain a clean driving record.

### The General Route (F-5-1)
This is for those who have lived in Korea for 5 consecutive years. While the 5-year requirement is manageable for many, the income requirement is steep: **Double the GNI** (approx. â‚©90.4 million). This category is often used by corporate executives or senior engineers who do not hold Korean degrees but have high earnings.

### The Graduate Route (F-5-10)
If you obtained a Bachelor's degree (in specific high-tech fields), a Master's, or a PhD from a South Korean university, this is your fast track.
*   **Requirements:** You must have held a job for 3 years (PhD holders may have shorter requirements) and be a regular employee.
*   **Income:** Only **1x GNI** is required.
*   **Benefit:** Recognizing your local education, the government makes the financial hurdle significantly lower.

### The Long-Term Resident Route (F-5-16)
This is currently the most popular route for professionals. If you have held an F-2-7 (Points-based) visa for 3 years, you can upgrade to F-5-16.
*   **Requirements:** You must maintain the F-2-7 status continuously for 3 years.
*   **Income:** **1x GNI**.
*   **Warning:** You must be employed at the time of application and usually need to show that you have been with your current company for at least 1 year to prove stability.

### Document Checklist
When you walk into the Sejong-ro or Omokgyo immigration office, your file should be thick. Missing a single paper can result in a "Supplement Request" which delays your application by weeks.

1.  **Application Form (Form 34)**
2.  **Passport & Alien Registration Card (ARC)**
3.  **Photo (3.5mm x 4.5mm, white background)**
4.  **Domestic Criminal Record Check:** (Immigration runs this internally, but you must sign a consent form).
5.  **Overseas Criminal Record Check:** Apostilled. Must be clean.
6.  **Proof of Income:** 'Certificate of Income Amount' (from Hometax) and Withholding Tax Receipt (from employer).
7.  **Proof of Employment:** Certificate of Employment and Copy of Company Business Registration.
8.  **KIIP Certificate:** Level 5 Permanent Residency Course Completion.
9.  **Housing Contract:** Proof of residence in your name or a generic residence form.
10. **Application Fee:** **â‚©200,000** (revenue stamps) + **â‚©30,000** (card fee).

## Maintaining Your Status

Once you receive that coveted F-5 card, there are very few requirements to keep it, but they are critical.

### Re-Entry Requirements
Previously, F-5 holders needed a re-entry permit if leaving for more than a year. Regulations have relaxed, and you can generally stay outside Korea for up to **2 years** without losing your residency status. However, if you plan to be gone longer than 2 years, you must apply for a re-entry permit; otherwise, your visa will be cancelled.

### Mandatory Card Renewal
Your legal status does not expire, but your physical card does. Every **10 years**, you must visit the immigration office to take a new photo and get a new card. This is strictly for identification updates, similar to renewing a driver's license. The fee is currently **â‚©30,000**.

## Common Pitfalls to Avoid

As an expat-focused platform, we have seen many applications rejected for preventable reasons. Here is what to watch out for in 2026.

**1. The "Income Gap" Trap**
Your income must be proven via the *Certificate of Income Amount* issued by the National Tax Service. This document for the previous year usually doesn't become available until **May**. If you apply in March using your company's internal pay stubs, immigration may reject it or put your application on hold until the official tax certificate is released.

**2. The Criminal Record Expiry**
Your overseas criminal record check is valid for only **3 months** (sometimes 6 months depending on officer discretion) from the date of issuance. If you request it too early and then delay your application to finish KIIP, your background check might expire, forcing you to restart that 8-week process.

**3. Changing Visa Types Mid-Process**
If you are on an E-7 and your contract ends while your F-5 is processing, you enter a "grey zone." You must ensure your current visa (E-7/F-2) remains valid *throughout* the F-5 processing period. Do not let your current visa expire assuming the F-5 will be approved in time.
