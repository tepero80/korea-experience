## The Legend of Jjimdak Alley

Andong Jjimdak is not an ancient dish from the Joseon Dynasty, despite the city's historic reputation. It emerged in the early 1980s in the Andong Old Market (Gu Market). Local merchants developed this dish to compete with the rising popularity of Western-style fried chicken franchises. By adding glass noodles, potatoes, carrots, and generous amounts of vegetables to the chicken to increase the volume, they created a meal that was both affordable and incredibly filling.

Today, the alley is a dense concentration of culinary history. Stretching approximately 180 meters, this narrow street houses over 30 specialized Jjimdak restaurants. Each shop claims to be the "original," but locals know that the subtle differences in sauce viscosity, spiciness (measured in Scoville heat units implicitly), and noodle texture make all the difference.

### The "Gonggibap" Strategy

Rice is rarely included in the main price; you must order it separately (â‚©1,000 per bowl). The pro move is not to eat all the rice with the chicken. Instead, save half your bowl of rice. When the chicken is finished and only the thick, savory soy sauce and broken bits of potatoes remain, dump your rice into the main platter and mix it. This "Bibim-bap" style ending is the culinary climax of the meal.

## How to Eat Like a Local

Eating Jjimdak is a messy, delicious affair. The glass noodles are incredibly long and slippery. If you try to lift them high into the air to put them in your mouth, you will likely splash dark soy sauce on your shirt.

## Beyond the Alley: What Else to See?

After consuming roughly 1,500 calories of delicious chicken, you will need to walk it off. Andong is known as the "Capital of Korean Spirit" and offers stunning cultural sites nearby.

### Hahoe Folk Village
Located 25 kilometers west of the market (about a 40-minute bus ride on Bus #246), this UNESCO World Heritage site is a must-visit. It costs â‚©5,000 for entry.

### Wolyeonggyo Bridge
Only 3.5 kilometers from the market, this is the longest wooden footbridge in Korea. It is best visited at sunset (around 19:30 in summer) when the bridge lights up and the fountain show begins. A taxi from Jjimdak Alley costs about â‚©5,500.

## Conclusion

Andong Jjimdak is more than just a meal; it is a testament to the resilience and creativity of the Andong market merchants. A trip to the Jjimdak Alley offers a sensory overload of sights, smells, and tastes that defines the Korean travel experience. Whether you choose the famous Hyundai, the traditional Yoojin, or the friendly Jongga, you are guaranteed a meal that is hearty, spicy, and unforgettable.

The next time you are planning a Korea itinerary, dedicate a day to Andong. Take the KTX, brave the heat of the Jjimdak Alley, and discover why this humble chicken dish conquered the nation.
