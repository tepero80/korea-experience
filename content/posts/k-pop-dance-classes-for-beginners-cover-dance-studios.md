## Essential Gear: What to Wear and Bring

The most common mistake international visitors make involves footwear. Korean studios are extremely strict about hygiene and floor maintenance.

**1. Indoor Shoes (Mandatory)**
You absolutely cannot wear the shoes you wore on the street into the dance studio. You must bring a separate pair of clean sneakers.
*   **The Rule:** Carry your dance shoes in a bag. Change into them in the locker room.
*   **Consequence:** If you forget, you will be forced to dance in your socks (slippery and dangerous) or buy cheap shoes nearby.

**2. Clothing Style**
K-Pop fashion is part of the experience.
*   **Trendy:** Baggy cargo pants, cropped tops, or oversized t-shirts are the norm.
*   **Avoid:** Rigid denim jeans or restrictive clothing. You need full range of motion.
*   **Colors:** Black, white, and grey are the most common colors in class, but don't be afraid to wear neon to stand out in the video.

## Navigating the "Vibe" and Etiquette

Korean culture relies heavily on unspoken rules, and the dance studio is no exception.

**1. Respecting Space:** Even in a crowded class, try not to invade the personal space of the dancer next to you. If you bump into someone, a quick bow or apology is expected.
**2. The Instructor:** When the instructor enters, the room usually goes quiet. It is respectful to clap after the demo performance.
**3. Phone Use:** It is generally acceptable to record yourself during break times, but do not record the instructor teaching the whole routine without permission, as this is their intellectual property.

<InfoBox type="warning" title="Video Etiquette">
Do not film other students without their consent. Many trainees attend these classes and are sensitive about their footage appearing on social media before their debut.
</InfoBox>
