## The Landscape: Understanding Korean Esports Structure

Koreaâ€™s dominance in gaming is built on speed and accessibility. With average internet speeds clocking in at 120 Mbps nationwide, the infrastructure supports a massive ecosystem. The scene is dominated by the LCK (League of Legends Champions Korea), which operates on a seasonal basis: Spring Split (January to April) and Summer Split (June to August).

Unlike Western tournaments that often rotate cities, the LCK regular season is stationary, held almost exclusively at LoL Park in central Seoul. This makes logistics easier for travelers, as you do not need to chase the tour across the country. However, major finals are often held in massive stadiums like the KSPO Dome or Gocheok Sky Dome, which can seat upwards of 15,000 to 20,000 spectators depending on the stage configuration.

## Major Venues: Where the Action Happens

Seoul hosts several dedicated esports arenas. Knowing exactly where these are located relative to your accommodation is crucial, as matches can run late into the evening, often past 10:00 PM KST.

### LoL Park (The Heart of LCK)
Located within the Gran Seoul building, LoL Park is an architectural marvel designed specifically for League of Legends. It features a circular arena where the audience surrounds the players, sitting less than 5 meters from the stage.

For fans of FPS games, the V.SPACE arena in the Dongdaemun area often hosts Valorant Champions Tour (VCT) Pacific matches. It offers a more industrial, darker aesthetic suited for shooter games. It is located roughly 3.5 kilometers east of LoL Park.

## StarCraft: The Legacy Lives On

While LoL is the current king, StarCraft: Brood War remains a religion in Korea. The ASL (AfreecaTV StarLeague) still draws crowds. These matches are typically held at the Afreeca Colosseum in Jamsil, located near the Lotte World Tower, roughly 12 kilometers southeast of the city center.

Tickets for ASL are generally easier to acquire than LCK tickets, often available ticket-free on a first-come, first-served basis for earlier rounds, or via TicketLink for finals. It provides a raw, nostalgic look at the roots of esports culture.

## Practical Tips for the Foreign Gamer

1.  **Navigation:** Use Naver Map or KakaoMap. Google Maps is unreliable for walking directions and transit times in Korea.
2.  **Payment:** Bring a T-Money card for transit and convenience stores. Most venues are cashless, accepting Visa/Mastercard, but smaller stalls may prefer cash.
3.  **Etiquette:** In the arena, you can cheer loudly during big plays, but silence is expected during tense tactical moments or interviews.
