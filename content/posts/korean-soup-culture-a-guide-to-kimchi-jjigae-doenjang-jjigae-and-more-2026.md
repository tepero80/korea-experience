## Understanding the Terminology: Guk vs. Tang vs. Jjigae

To navigate a Korean menu effectively, you must first distinguish between the three main categories of liquid dishes. While they may look similar to the untrained eye, the ratio of liquid to solid ingredients and the method of serving differ significantly.

You might also enjoy our article about [Korean Soup Culture Guide Gamjatang Galbitang Seolleongtang](/blog/korean-soup-culture-gamjatang-galbitang-seolleongtang).

**Guk** has the highest liquid-to-solid ratio. It is usually lighter, served in individual bowls, and accompanies rice. **Tang** is a more formal term for soup, often requiring longer cooking times (8 to 12 hours for bone broths) and is frequently shared or served in larger earthenware pots. **Jjigae** is the thickest of the three, seasoned heavily with chili paste or soybean paste, and is undeniably the most popular choice for a hearty lunch.

## The Holy Trinity of Korean Stews

When visiting Korea in 2026, three specific stews dominate the culinary landscape. These are available in roughly 90% of general Korean restaurants and constitute the daily fuel for millions of locals.

### 2. Doenjang Jjigae (Soybean Paste Stew)

If Kimchi Jjigae is the fiery younger sibling, Doenjang Jjigae is the wise, earthy elder. Made from fermented soybean paste (doenjang), this stew is savory, salty, and complex. It usually contains zucchini, tofu, onions, and sometimes brisket or clams.

Historically, doenjang has been a source of protein in Korea for over 2,000 years. The stew is lighter on the stomach than its spicy counterparts and is often the standard accompaniment to Korean BBQ. A standard serving contains roughly 15-20 grams of protein, making it a nutritious option.

### 3. Sundubu Jjigae (Soft Tofu Stew)

Sundubu Jjigae features uncurdled, extra-soft tofu that has a silken texture. This stew is typically cooked in a small earthenware pot called a *ttukbaegi*, which retains heat for 15 to 20 minutes after arriving at the table. It is famous for the raw egg cracked into the boiling broth just before servingâ€”the residual heat cooks the egg to a perfect poach in about 2 minutes.

## Soups for Stamina and Hangovers

Korean culture has a specific soup for every physical condition. Whether you are draining energy in the summer heat or recovering from a night of soju, there is a broth prescribed for you.

### Samgyetang: The Summer Stamina Booster

Samgyetang is a whole young chicken stuffed with glutinous rice, ginseng, jujube (red dates), and garlic, floating in a rich broth. It is traditionally eaten during *Sambok*, the three hottest days of the lunar calendar (typically July and August).

In 2026, the price of a premium Samgyetang in Seoul averages between 18,000 and â‚©25,000 ($13-19 USD). The ginseng used is typically 3 to 4 years old, believed to boost the immune system and increase energy.

### Haejangguk: The Hangover Cure

The word *Haejangguk* literally translates to "soup to chase a hangover." This is a category rather than a single dish, but the most common version features a beef broth with napa cabbage, bean sprouts, and congealed ox blood (seonji). If ox blood sounds intimidating, you can opt for *Byeo-Haejangguk* (Bone Hangover Soup), which features pork spine meat that falls off the bone.

These restaurants often operate 24 hours a day. A typical bowl costs 9,000 to â‚©11,000 ($7-8 USD) and is heavily seasoned to shock the system back into operation.

## The Milky Broths: Seolleongtang and Gomtang

For those who dislike spice, the milky white bone broths are a culinary sanctuary. 

**Seolleongtang** is made by boiling ox leg bones for 12 to 24 hours until the calcium is extracted, turning the broth a milky white. It is served with brisket slices and somyeon (thin wheat noodles). **Gomtang** is similar but often clearer as it includes more meat and fewer bones in the boiling process.

Crucially, these soups are served unseasoned. You will find sea salt, black pepper, and chopped green onions at the table. It is up to you to season it to your taste. A typical serving contains about 800mg of sodium before you add salt, so taste it first.

## Army Stew (Budae Jjigae): A Modern Classic

Budae Jjigae is a fusion dish born out of necessity after the Korean War. It combines Korean staples like gochujang (chili paste) and kimchi with US military surplus items like Spam, hot dogs, and baked beans. Today, it is a beloved comfort food, often topped with processed cheese and instant ramen noodles.

It is a communal dish, almost always cooked at the table on a portable gas burner. A medium pot for two people usually costs around â‚©24,000 ($18 USD). It is rich, caloric (approx. 700 kcal per serving), and incredibly satisfying.

## Dining Etiquette and Tips for 2026

Dining in Korea involves specific protocols, especially regarding soups and stews.

### 1. The Spoon is King
Unlike in some other Asian cultures where the bowl is lifted to the mouth, in Korea, the bowl stays on the table. You use the long-handled spoon (*sujeo*) to drink the broth. Using chopsticks to fish out solid ingredients is acceptable, but drinking soup directly from the bowl is generally frowned upon in formal settings.

### 2. Communal vs. Individual
In 2026, hygiene awareness has shifted dining habits slightly. While older generations may still dip spoons into a communal pot (*jjigae*), most restaurants now provide a ladle and individual small bowls (*ap-jeopsi*). Always use the ladle to transfer the stew to your personal bowl before eating.

### 3. The "Rice Lid" Trick
When eating hot soup, you can remove the stainless steel lid of your rice bowl, flip it over, and use it as a cooling plate for hot tofu or meat taken from the boiling stew. This is a common practice among locals to avoid burning the tongue.
