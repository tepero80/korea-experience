## Understanding the "Red Gold": Types of Korean Ginseng

Before you open your wallet, you must understand the hierarchy of ginseng. Not all roots are created equal, and the processing method drastically changes the chemical profile, specifically the ginsenosides (active compounds). In 2026, the market is strictly regulated, but confusion still exists for non-Korean speakers.

### Fresh, White, and Red Ginseng

**Fresh Ginseng (Susam):** This is the raw root harvested directly from the field. It is approximately 75% water and spoils easily, making it difficult to transport internationally. It is mostly used for cooking (like in Samgyetang chicken soup) or making alcohol.

**White Ginseng (Baeksam):** This is fresh ginseng that has been peeled and dried in the sun. It has a lower moisture content (under 14%) and can be stored for long periods. It is milder and often used in herbal teas.

**Red Ginseng (Hongsam):** This is the gold standard for health supplements. Fresh ginseng is steamed and then dried, which turns the root a reddish-brown color. This process locks in the nutrients and actually creates new bioactive compounds that don't exist in the raw root. It is the most potent form for energy and immunity.

<InfoBox type="tip" title="Pro Tip">
When buying gifts, always look for "6-Year-Grown" on the label. Ginseng reaches its peak nutritional density at year six. Roots harvested at year four contain significantly fewer saponins, while roots older than seven years tend to become woody and lose quality.
</InfoBox>

### The Science of Saponins
The active ingredients in ginseng are called saponins, or ginsenosides. Korean Red Ginseng is unique because it contains over 30 varieties of saponins, compared to American ginseng (14 varieties) or Chinese ginseng (15 varieties). In 2026, premium products are legally required to list the content of specific ginsenosides: Rg1, Rb1, and Rg3.
