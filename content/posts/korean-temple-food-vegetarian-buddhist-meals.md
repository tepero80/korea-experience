## What Defines Korean Temple Food?

Korean temple food, known locally as *Sachal Eamsik*, is built on the Buddhist philosophy of non-violence and mindfulness. It is not just about what you eat, but how the food is grown, prepared, and consumed. The cuisine dates back to 372 AD when Buddhism was first introduced to the Korean peninsula. Today, approximately 17% of South Korea's population identifies as Buddhist, yet this cuisine is enjoyed by food lovers regardless of religion due to its health benefits and clean flavors.

Related reading: [Vegan and vegetarian guide to Korean food 2026 essential ...](/blog/vegan-and-vegetarian-guide-to-korean-food-2026).

The fundamental principle is that food is medicine for the body and mind. Chefs use natural seasonings like kelp, mushroom powder, and wild sesame seeds rather than artificial enhancers. A typical meal consists of 5 to 7 side dishes (*banchan*), a soup, and rice, totaling around 400-600 calories per standard serving, making it significantly lighter than average restaurant fare.

### The Forbidden Five (Oshinchae)
The most distinct characteristic of temple food is the absence of *Oshinchae*, or the five pungent vegetables. While garlic and onions are the backbone of secular Korean cooking, they are strictly banned in temples.

You might also enjoy our article about [Best food apps for English speakers TableCheck vs CatchTable](/blog/best-food-apps-for-english-speakers-tablecheck-vs-catchtable-2026).

<InfoBox type="note" title="Why No Garlic?">
Buddhists believe that these five strong spices create excessive heat in the body. If eaten raw, they are said to disturb the temper; if eaten cooked, they are believed to act as an aphrodisiac, disturbing the calm mind required for meditation.
</InfoBox>

The forbidden ingredients are:
1.  **Garlic**
2.  **Green Onions (Scallions)**
3.  **Wild Chives**
4.  **Korean Leeks**
5.  **Onions**

Instead, flavor is built using *Doenjang* (soybean paste), *Ganjang* (soy sauce), and *Gochujang* (chili paste), alongside turmeric, Sichuan pepper (*Zanthoxylum*), and shiitake mushrooms.

## Top Temple Food Restaurants in Seoul

You do not need to hike up a mountain to experience high-end temple cuisine. Seoul is home to several world-class restaurants, including one that has held a Michelin star. These venues offer course meals ranging from 45,000 KRW to 150,000 KRW ($35 to $115 USD).

### 1. Balwoo Gongyang
Run directly by the Jogye Order of Korean Buddhism, this is the gold standard for temple cuisine in the city. Located on the 5th floor of the Temple Stay Information Center across from Jogyesa Temple, it offers a serene atmosphere despite being in the city center.

## The Authentic Experience: Temple Stays

For travelers who want to go beyond a restaurant meal, a Temple Stay program offers the most authentic way to experience this food. You will eat exactly what the monks eat, often in communal silence. There are over 130 temples in Korea operating these programs, with about 25 in the Seoul metropolitan area.

### Jingwansa Temple
Located in Bukhansan National Park, roughly 45 minutes from central Seoul, Jingwansa is famous for its fermentation jars. The nuns here are renowned for their culinary skills. A "Templestay" here typically includes a tea ceremony and a formal monastic meal.

*   **Cost:** Overnight stay approx. 70,000 - 100,000 KRW ($54 - $77).
*   **Day Program:** Approx. 50,000 KRW ($38) for 4 hours.

This is the pilgrimage site for foodies. Located in Naejangsan National Park (about 3 hours south of Seoul by KTX train + taxi), this is where the famous nun Jeong Kwan cooks.

## Seasonal Menus and Ingredients

Because temple food relies entirely on foraging and farming, the menu is strictly dictated by the seasons. Understanding this calendar helps you know what to expect on your plate.

### Spring (March - May)
Spring is the season of greens and shoots. The mountains are lush with edible herbs.
*   **Key Ingredients:** Mugwort, wild parsley, bamboo shoots, fatsia shoots.
*   **Dish Highlight:** Mugwort soup (*Ssuk-guk*)â€”earthy and slightly bitter, known for detoxifying the liver.

### Summer (June - August)
Korea's summers are hot and humid (average 30Â°C / 86Â°F). The food focuses on cooling the body.
*   **Key Ingredients:** Eggplant, cucumber, summer squash, potatoes.
*   **Dish Highlight:** Cold noodle soup with soybean broth (*Kong-guksu*). It provides high protein without the heaviness of meat.

### Autumn (September - November)
The harvest season. This is when ingredients are dried and preserved for winter.
*   **Key Ingredients:** Mushrooms (Pine mushrooms are a luxury), root vegetables, ginkgo nuts, persimmons.
*   **Dish Highlight:** Deep-fried mushrooms glazed with grain syrup.

### Winter (December - February)
Fresh produce is scarce, so the diet shifts to fermented foods and dried greens prepared earlier in the year.
*   **Key Ingredients:** Kimchi (vegan version without salted shrimp), dried radish greens (*Siraegi*), seaweed, tofu.
*   **Dish Highlight:** Dumplings (*Mandu*) stuffed with tofu and kimchi.

## Is It Worth the Price?

For travelers on a budget, paying $80+ for a vegetable meal might seem steep. However, the value lies in the labor intensity. A single drop of soy sauce used in your dressing might have been tended to for 5 years. The wild herbs were likely hand-picked in the mountains at dawn.
