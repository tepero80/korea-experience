## What Exactly is Jeonse?

The Jeonse system is a housing lease arrangement unique to South Korea. Instead of paying a landlord monthly rent, the tenant provides a large depositâ€”typically between **50% to 80% of the property's sale price**â€”for a fixed contract period, usually two years.

The landlord invests this capital to earn interest or uses it for other investments during the lease term. At the end of the contract, the landlord returns the principal amount to the tenant in full. Essentially, you are lending the landlord money interest-free in exchange for living in their property rent-free.

## Jeonse vs. Wolse: The Financial Breakdown

The alternative to Jeonse is **Wolse** (Monthly Rent). Wolse involves a smaller deposit (Key Money) and a monthly payment. However, "smaller" is relative; Wolse deposits in Korea are still significantly higher than the 1-2 months' rent common in the West, often ranging from **10 million to â‚©50 million**.

### Which is Better for You?

The choice depends on your liquidity. If you have significant capital or access to low-interest loans, Jeonse is mathematically cheaper because the "cost" is effectively the interest you would have earned on that money (or the interest you pay on a loan), which is usually lower than monthly rent.

## Can Foreigners Get Jeonse Loans?

Yes, but eligibility has tightened slightly in 2026 to prevent household debt inflation. Generally, foreigners on short-term visas (tourist or student visas without an Alien Registration Card) must pay in cash. However, residents with long-term visas can access banking products.

### Visa Eligibility
*   **F-Series Visas (F-2, F-4, F-5, F-6):** Most banks treat these residents similarly to Korean citizens. You can often borrow up to **80% of the Jeonse deposit**.
*   **E-Series Visas (E-7, etc.):** Some specific bank branches (like Shinhan or Hana Bank global centers) offer products, usually capped at **200 million to â‚©400 million**.

### Interest Rates 2026
Current Jeonse loan interest rates fluctuate between **3.8% and 4.9%** depending on your credit score and the bank. While higher than the 2% rates seen in 2020, this is still often cheaper than paying pure monthly rent in high-demand areas like Gangnam or Mapo.

## Safety First: Avoiding Jeonse Fraud

The biggest risk for foreigners is "Jeonse Fraud" (often called "Tin-can Jeonse" or *Kkangtong Jeonse*). This happens when the landlord owes more on the house (mortgage + your deposit) than the house is worth. If the house is auctioned off, you might lose your deposit.

### Essential Safety Checks for 2026

1.  **Check the Certified Copy of Real Estate Register (Deunggi-bu Deungbon):** Ensure the landlord's debt ratio is safe. Ideally, the mortgage plus your deposit should not exceed **70%** of the house's market value.
2.  **Jeonse Insurance (HUG):** It is highly recommended to sign up for Jeonse Return Guarantee Insurance via HUG (Korea Housing & Urban Guarantee Corporation). This costs roughly **0.12% - 0.15%** of your deposit annually but guarantees you get your money back.

<InfoBox type="danger" title="CRITICAL: The Fixed Date">
  Immediately after signing your contract (or on moving day), you must visit the local community center (Dong-office) to get the "Fixed Date" (Hwak-jeong-il-ja) stamped on your contract and report your move-in. This gives you legal priority over creditors if the landlord goes bankrupt. Do not delay this by even one day.
</InfoBox>
