## Introduction: Decoding Korea's Recycling Maze for New Expats

Welcome to South Korea, a land of captivating culture, cutting-edge technology, and an incredibly efficient yet often perplexing waste management system. For new expats, one of the first and most frequent challenges encountered isn't the language barrier or understanding public transport, but rather the seemingly intricate world of **Recycling in Korea**. What might appear as a simple task in your home country often transforms into a complicated multi-step process here, involving specific bags, precise sorting, and designated collection days.

The Korean government has implemented a robust waste management system driven by environmental consciousness and resource efficiency. This system, while highly effective, demands a significant learning curve from its residents, particularly those unfamiliar with its nuances. From differentiating between types of plastics to understanding the proper disposal of food waste, the rules can feel overwhelming. Without proper guidance, expats often find themselves standing confused in front of overflowing bins, unsure if their efforts are compliant.

This comprehensive guide for 2026 aims to demystify **Recycling in Korea** for new expats. We will break down the complex rules into digestible segments, provide practical tips, illustrate common pitfalls, and offer a clear roadmap to becoming a responsible and eco-conscious resident. By the end of this article, you will not only understand *how* to recycle in Korea but also *why* it's done this way, empowering you to navigate the system with confidence and ease. Let's embark on this journey to a cleaner, greener Korea together.

## Understanding Korea's Waste Management Philosophy

South Korea's approach to waste management is rooted in a strong national commitment to sustainability, resource conservation, and reducing landfill reliance. This commitment has led to a highly regulated system that emphasizes source separation, recycling, and composting. Unlike some countries where waste is simply thrown into a general bin, Korea expects residents to actively participate in sorting their refuse into very specific categories. This philosophy isn't just about convenience; it's about extending the life cycle of materials and minimizing environmental impact.

The core principle behind **Recycling in Korea** is "volume-based waste fee system" (종량제 봉투 – Jongnyangje Bongtu), introduced in 1995. This system mandates that residents purchase specific, government-issued garbage bags for general waste and food waste. The cost of these bags directly incorporates the waste disposal fee, incentivizing people to reduce their general waste and recycle more, as recycling itself is generally free (with some exceptions like bulky waste). This direct financial incentive, coupled with public awareness campaigns, has significantly increased recycling rates and reduced the volume of waste sent to landfills.

Furthermore, Korea takes a proactive stance on extended producer responsibility, meaning manufacturers are often held accountable for the recycling and disposal of their products. This influences packaging design and encourages the use of recyclable materials. The result is a system that, while demanding, significantly contributes to Korea's environmental goals, making it a key aspect of daily life for every resident, including expats. Embracing this philosophy is the first step towards successfully navigating the country's waste disposal requirements.

## The Essential Four: Separating Your Recyclables

When it comes to **Recycling in Korea**, the vast majority of items fall into specific categories that require careful separation. Mastering these "Essential Four" and their nuances is crucial for any expat. Remember, improper sorting can lead to your recyclables being rejected or, in some cases, fines.

### 1. Paper and Cardboard (종이류 – Jong-i-ryu)

This category includes newspapers, magazines, books, cardboard boxes, paper packaging, and milk cartons.
*   **Newspapers/Magazines/Books:** Stack neatly, tie with string or place in a paper bag. Remove any plastic covers or bindings.
*   **Cardboard Boxes:** Flatten them completely. Remove any plastic tape, labels, or metal staples. Large boxes often need to be broken down.
*   **Paper Packaging:** Remove any food residue. If it's coated paper (like some frozen food boxes), it may need to be treated as general waste if the coating is inseparable.
*   **Milk Cartons/Beverage Cartons:** Rinse them thoroughly, open them flat, and dry them. Collect them separately from other paper. Some districts have specific collection points for these due to their higher quality pulp.

### 2. Plastics (플라스틱류 – Peullaseutik-ryu)

This is one of the most confusing categories due to the various types of plastic. Look for the recycling symbols (numbers 1-7) on the packaging.
*   **PET (01), HDPE (02), PP (05), PS (06), PVC (03), LDPE (04), OTHER (07):** Generally, plastic bottles (water, soda), plastic food containers, and detergent bottles fall here.
*   **Preparation:** Rinse bottles and containers thoroughly to remove any food or liquid residue. Remove labels, caps, and pumps. Caps, if made of a different plastic or metal, often need to be separated and either recycled separately (if indicated) or disposed of as general waste. Crush bottles to save space.
*   **Vinyl (비닐류 – Binil-ryu):** Often confused with general plastics, thin plastic bags, snack wrappers, noodle packaging, and cling film (랩 – laep) are classified as "vinyl." These are collected separately from rigid plastics. They must be clean and dry. If they have food residue and cannot be cleaned, they go into general waste. Some areas ask for these to be bundled together, others allow them loose.

### 3. Glass (유리병류 – Yu-ri-byeong-ryu)

Glass bottles are collected for recycling.
*   **Types:** Clear, brown, and green glass bottles (e.g., soju, beer, beverage bottles).
*   **Preparation:** Remove caps (metal caps often go with metal, plastic caps often general waste, unless specified). Rinse bottles thoroughly. Labels usually do not need to be removed unless they are very thick plastic.
*   **What NOT to include:** Broken glass (danger to workers – general waste in a thick bag, labeled), ceramic items, porcelain, mirrors, window panes, heat-resistant glass (e.g., Pyrex), light bulbs. These items are *not* recyclable with standard glass and usually belong in general waste or bulky waste.

### 4. Metals (고철류 – Go-cheol-ryu)

This category includes aluminum cans, steel cans, and other small metal items.
*   **Types:** Beverage cans (soda, beer), food cans (tuna, spam), metal lids, aluminum foil (clean).
*   **Preparation:** Rinse cans thoroughly to remove any food residue. Crush cans to save space. Remove plastic elements if possible.
*   **What NOT to include:** Gas canisters (highly dangerous – must be emptied completely, punctured if safe, and handled as special waste or disposed of in designated collection bins at specific centers, never general recycling), batteries (separate collection).

| :----------------- | :------------------------------------------------------ | :----------------------------------------------------- |
| **Paper**          | Newspapers, magazines, cardboard (flattened, no tape)    | Waxed paper, photos, food-stained paper, receipts      |

Understanding these distinctions and preparing your items correctly will make **Recycling in Korea** a much smoother process.

## General Waste (종량제 봉투 - Jongnyangje Bongtu) and Food Waste (음식물 쓰레기 - Eumsikmul Sseuregi)

Beyond the main recyclable categories, effectively managing general waste and food waste is equally critical for expats in Korea. These two categories have very specific disposal methods and requirements.

### General Waste: The Standard Garbage Bag (종량제 봉투 – Jongnyangje Bongtu)

General waste, also known as "burnable" or "landfill" waste, refers to anything that cannot be recycled in the specific categories outlined above, or items that are too contaminated to be recycled. This includes items like:
*   Non-recyclable plastics (e.g., toothbrushes, plastic toys, styrofoam if food-stained)
*   Ceramics, porcelain, broken glass (wrapped safely)
*   Diapers, sanitary pads
*   Dust, small debris
*   Small amount of contaminated food packaging that cannot be cleaned
*   Pet waste (double-bagged)
*   Receipts (due to thermal paper coating)
*   Chewing gum, cigarette butts

**The Jongnyangje Bongtu System:** You must purchase special, municipality-issued **Jongnyangje Bongtu** (standard garbage bags) for all general waste. These bags come in various sizes (e.g., 5L, 10L, 20L, 50L, 100L) and are typically light blue or white. The price of the bag covers the cost of collection and disposal.
*   **Where to Buy:** You can purchase these bags at most convenience stores (e.g., GS25, CU, 7-Eleven), supermarkets (e.g., E-Mart, Lotte Mart, Homeplus), and even some smaller grocery stores. Just ask for "쓰레기 봉투" (sseuregi bongtu).
*   **Cost (approximate for 2026):**
    *   10L: 250-300 KRW
    *   20L: 490-600 KRW
    *   50L: 1,200-1,500 KRW
*   **Disposal:** Once full, tie the bag securely and place it in the designated general waste collection area or bin on your building's specified collection day.

### Food Waste: The Dedicated System (음식물 쓰레기 – Eumsikmul Sseuregi)

Food waste in Korea is collected separately for composting or animal feed. This is one of the most strictly enforced and unique aspects of **Recycling in Korea**. The goal is to reduce food waste sent to landfills, where it produces harmful methane gas.

**What IS Food Waste:**
*   Cooked food scraps (e.g., leftover rice, noodles, vegetables, meat scraps, bread)
*   Fruit and vegetable peels (e.g., apple cores, banana peels)
*   Small bones (chicken, fish bones – generally acceptable)
*   Fish guts and scales

**What is NOT Food Waste (and goes into General Waste Jongnyangje Bongtu):**
*   Large bones (pork, beef bones)
*   Seafood shells (clams, oysters, crabs, shrimp shells)
*   Eggshells
*   Tea bags, coffee grounds (though some districts might allow coffee grounds with food waste, check locally)
*   Nutshells (e.g., peanut shells, walnut shells)
*   Fruit pits (e.g., peach, persimmon, avocado pits)
*   Hard vegetable parts (e.g., corn cobs, outer layers of onion, garlic peels)
*   Plastic, toothpicks, other foreign objects.

**The Eumsikmul Sseuregi System:** Similar to general waste, you must use special, municipality-issued **Eumsikmul Sseuregi** (food waste) bags. These bags are often yellow or pink and are typically smaller in size (e.g., 1L, 2L, 3L, 5L, 10L) due to the weight and smell of food waste.
*   **Where to Buy:** Same locations as general waste bags (convenience stores, supermarkets). Ask for "음식물 쓰레기 봉투" (eumsikmul sseuregi bongtu).
*   **Cost (approximate for 2026):**
    *   3L: 100-150 KRW
    *   5L: 150-250 KRW
    *   10L: 250-400 KRW
*   **Disposal Methods:**
    1.  **Special Bags:** Fill the special food waste bag, tie it securely to prevent spills and odors, and deposit it in the designated food waste bin in your building or community on collection day.
    2.  **RFID Bins:** In many newer apartment complexes and urban areas, an RFID (Radio-Frequency Identification) system is used. You deposit your food waste into a specialized bin, and the machine weighs your waste and charges you based on weight. You usually use a special card (often linked to your T-Money or apartment card) to operate these bins. No special bags are needed for these. This method is becoming increasingly common.
*   **Tips:** Store food waste in the freezer before disposal to minimize odor and pests, especially during warmer months. Drain excess liquid before placing it in the bag.

Mastering both general and food waste disposal is a cornerstone of living responsibly in Korea. It takes practice, but soon becomes second nature.

## Bulky Waste (대형 폐기물 - Daehyeong Pyegimul) and Special Items

Beyond the daily grind of sorting, expats will eventually encounter items that don't fit into regular recycling or general waste categories. This is where bulky waste and other special items come into play, requiring specific procedures.

### Bulky Waste (대형 폐기물 – Daehyeong Pyegimul)

Bulky waste refers to large items that cannot fit into standard Jongnyangje Bongtu bags. This includes furniture (sofas, tables, chairs), large appliances (refrigerators, washing machines), mattresses, bicycles, and large electronics.
*   **Disposal Method:** You cannot simply leave these items on the street. You must purchase a special sticker or declaration form for each item.
    1.  **Online Application:** The most convenient method is to apply and pay online through your local district office (Gu-cheong – 구청) website. Search for "대형 폐기물 인터넷 신고" (daehyeong pyegimul inteonet singo). You'll typically need to input your address, select the item from a list (each item has a specific fee), pay online, and then print a confirmation sticker or write down the declaration number.
    2.  **In-Person Purchase:** Alternatively, you can visit your local Gu-cheong (district office) or a community service center (동사무소 / 주민센터 – Dongsamuso / Jumin Center) to purchase physical stickers. You then attach the sticker to the item.
*   **Fees (approximate for 2026):** Fees vary significantly by item and district.
    *   Chair: 3,000-5,000 KRW
    *   Small table: 5,000-10,000 KRW
    *   Single bed mattress: 10,000-20,000 KRW
    *   Sofa (3-seater): 20,000-50,000 KRW
    *   Large refrigerator: 30,000-70,000 KRW
*   **Collection:** After attaching the sticker or writing the declaration number, place the item at the designated bulky waste collection point (often near your building's regular waste area, or a visible spot on the street) on the scheduled collection day (or sometimes you can arrange a specific pick-up date). Do NOT place it out too early.

### Electronic Waste (E-Waste)

Small electronics like toasters or hair dryers often go into general waste, but larger items require specific disposal.
*   **Large Appliances (Refrigerators, TVs, Washing Machines, Air Conditioners):** These can often be collected for free through a dedicated service. Call the free collection service at **1599-0903** or visit their website (www.15990903.or.kr) to schedule a pick-up. This service is designed to safely dismantle and recycle components.
*   **Small Appliances (Computer monitors, Printers, Vacuum Cleaners):** Some districts have specific collection days or designated bins for small e-waste. Check your local Gu-cheong website. Often, if you're disposing of a large appliance, you can request that they also pick up a few small ones simultaneously.
*   **Batteries:** Do not throw batteries into general waste or regular recycling. They contain hazardous materials. Look for dedicated battery collection boxes, often found at apartment complex entrances, convenience stores, or Gu-cheong buildings.

### Clothes and Textile Waste (의류수거함 – Eui-ryu-su-geo-ham)

Clothing, fabrics, shoes, and bags can often be donated or recycled.
*   **Collection Bins:** Look for large, green or blue metal bins (의류수거함) placed around residential areas. These are for clean, usable clothes, shoes (tied together in pairs), and bags.
*   **What NOT to include:** Dirty or torn clothes, bedding (duvets, pillows), underwear, rags, carpets, stuffed toys. These typically go into general waste (if small) or bulky waste (if large).

### Hazardous Waste

*   **Fluorescent Light Bulbs:** These contain mercury. Collect them separately and deposit them in designated collection boxes, often found at apartment complex recycling areas or community centers.
*   **Medical Waste (needles, syringes):** Consult with your local pharmacy or hospital for proper disposal methods. Never put these in general waste.
*   **Chemicals, Paints:** Contact your local Gu-cheong for instructions on hazardous chemical waste disposal.

Handling these special items correctly ensures compliance and promotes a safer, cleaner environment. Always check your local district's specific guidelines, as rules can vary slightly.

## Navigating Recycling in Different Living Situations

The specifics of **Recycling in Korea** can vary significantly depending on where you live. Whether you're in a high-rise apartment, a multi-family villa, or a detached house, understanding your building's or neighborhood's system is key.

### 1. Apartment Complexes (아파트 – Apateu)

Apartment complexes typically have the most organized and convenient recycling systems.
*   **Centralized Recycling Zones:** Most complexes have a designated area, often in the basement or a separate building, for residents to drop off their sorted recyclables. This area usually has clearly marked bins for each category: paper, plastic, vinyl, glass, metal, and sometimes separate bins for milk cartons, styrofoam, and batteries.
*   **Collection Schedule:** Recyclables are often collected daily or several times a week. General waste and food waste typically have specific collection days, sometimes every day, other times 2-3 times a week. Check with your building management office (관리사무소 – Gwan-li-samu-so) or notice boards for the exact schedule.
*   **Food Waste:** Modern apartments often use the RFID system for food waste, where you weigh your waste at a communal machine using a smart card. Older complexes might still require special food waste bags.
*   **Bulky Waste:** Usually, there's a designated spot for bulky waste, often collected on specific days after you've purchased and attached the sticker.
*   **Advantages:** Convenience, clear labeling, usually indoor or covered facilities, and on-site staff who can answer questions.
*   **Tips:** Take a photo of the recycling signs in your building for quick reference. Don't be afraid to ask a neighbor or the building manager for clarification.

### 2. Villas and Officetels (빌라/오피스텔 – Billa/Opiseutel)

"Villas" (multi-family residential buildings, usually 3-5 stories) and officetels (combined office/residential buildings) often have a less centralized system than large apartment complexes, but more structured than single houses.
*   **Street-Side Collection:** Recycling bins (often large mesh containers or designated collection points) for paper, plastics, glass, and metal are typically located on the street near the building or a designated communal spot for several buildings. These are usually collected on specific days of the week.
*   **General Waste and Food Waste:** You'll use Jongnyangje Bongtu and Eumsikmul Sseuregi bags, placing them in designated bins or directly on the street at the collection point on the specified days. Times are often from evening (e.g., 6 PM) on the day before collection, until the early morning collection.
*   **Collection Schedule:** It's crucial to learn the specific collection days for your street or neighborhood. Your landlord, real estate agent, or neighbors are the best sources of information. Look for signs posted on streetlights or utility poles, or use local district office resources.
*   **Bulky Waste:** Same procedure as apartments; purchase a sticker and place it in a visible spot on the designated collection day.
*   **Challenges:** Less clear signage, potential for confusion regarding collection days, exposure to weather.
*   **Tips:** Ask your landlord for a detailed recycling schedule. Observe your neighbors for a few days to understand the routine. Misplaced waste is more visible and can lead to complaints or fines.

### 3. Detached Houses (단독주택 – Dandokjutaek)

Residents of detached houses usually have the most direct responsibility for their waste disposal.
*   **Street-Side Collection:** All types of waste – sorted recyclables, general waste bags, and food waste bags – are placed directly on the street or sidewalk in front of your house or a designated communal spot.
*   **Collection Schedule:** Strict adherence to collection days and times is essential. Different types of waste will have different collection days (e.g., Monday/Wednesday for general waste, Tuesday/Thursday for recyclables, etc.).
*   **No Centralized Bins:** Unlike apartments, there are usually no large communal bins. You'll place your sorted and bagged items directly on the street.
*   **Bulky Waste:** Same procedure – sticker purchase and placement on the street.
*   **Challenges:** No building management to guide you, high visibility of misplaced waste.
*   **Tips:** Your local Gu-cheong website or a quick visit to the Dongsamuso (community center) is the best way to get a detailed, official schedule for your specific address. Befriend a neighbor to clarify any doubts. Always place your waste out *on* the collection day evening, not days in advance.

## Common Pitfalls and Pro Tips for Expats

Even with a good understanding of the rules, new expats often fall into common traps when it comes to **Recycling in Korea**. Here's how to avoid them and some insider tips for a smoother experience.

### Common Pitfalls to Avoid:

1.  **"Just Throwing It All in General Waste":** While tempting when confused, this is not only wasteful but also disrespectful to local customs and potentially leads to fines. Korea's system is built on collective effort.
2.  **Not Cleaning/Separating Properly:** A plastic bottle with liquid still inside, a pizza box with grease stains, or a glass bottle with the lid still on – these are common mistakes. Contaminated items often end up in general waste, negating your recycling effort.
3.  **Ignoring Collection Days/Times:** Placing waste out on the wrong day or too early/late can result in your trash being left on the street, attracting pests, and leading to complaints from neighbors or building managers. Fines can also be imposed.
4.  **Misidentifying Materials:** Confusing vinyl packaging with rigid plastic, or putting ceramic in glass recycling. This contamination makes the recycling process inefficient.
5.  **Assuming Universal Rules:** Believing that recycling rules are the same across all districts or even buildings. They can vary slightly, especially regarding minor items like coffee grounds or plastic bottle caps.

### Pro Tips for Expats:

1.  **Get a Local Schedule:** The absolute first step. For apartments, ask your building manager. For villas/houses, check your local Gu-cheong website (they often have English sections or guides) or visit the Dongsamuso (community center) with a translator app. Write down or print out the schedule and stick it on your fridge.
2.  **Observe Your Neighbors:** When in doubt, discreetly observe what your neighbors do. This is a common and effective way to learn local customs for waste disposal.
3.  **Invest in Multiple Bins:** Having 4-5 small bins at home for different categories (general, food, paper, plastics, glass/metal) makes daily sorting much easier.
4.  **Keep a "Dirty Plastics/Paper" Bin:** For items that *look* recyclable but are too contaminated to clean (e.g., oily plastic containers, heavily stained paper), have a dedicated small bin that goes directly into general waste. This prevents contamination of your true recyclables.
5.  **Use a Translation App:** When looking at packaging, use Google Translate or Papago on your phone to identify material types if the English is absent or unclear.
6.  **"One Small Bag Rule":** For general and food waste, try to fill up one small bag at a time. This helps you monitor your waste production and also prevents bags from getting too heavy or smelly.
7.  **Know Your Gu-cheong (District Office):** The Gu-cheong is your ultimate resource for official information regarding all waste disposal rules, bulky waste stickers, and reporting issues. Many now offer multilingual services.
8.  **Internal Linking Tip:** For even more specific guidance on household management, including setting up utility accounts and understanding local services, check out our guide: "Essential Utilities and Services for Expats in Korea."
9.  **Don't Be Afraid to Ask:** Koreans are generally very helpful. If you're genuinely trying and ask politely, locals or your landlord will likely be happy to guide you. It shows you're committed to adapting.
10. **Embrace Technology:** Search for local recycling apps (though many are in Korean, some provide visual aids). Some apartment complexes have apps that integrate waste schedules.

By being proactive and mindful of these tips, you'll master **Recycling in Korea** in no time and contribute positively to your new community.

## Embracing a Greener Lifestyle in Korea

Beyond simply following the rules, embracing **Recycling in Korea** is an excellent opportunity to adopt a more environmentally conscious lifestyle. Korea's robust system encourages residents to think critically about their consumption and waste generation. Here's how you can take it a step further:

### Reduce, Reuse, Recycle: The Core Principles

*   **Reduce Consumption:** The most impactful step is to reduce the amount of waste you generate in the first place.
    *   **Reusable Bags:** Always carry a reusable shopping bag (장바구니 – jangbaguni) when grocery shopping. Many stores charge for plastic bags.
    *   **Reusable Cups/Tumblers:** Many coffee shops offer discounts (typically 200-500 KRW) if you bring your own reusable cup.
    *   **Minimize Packaging:** Opt for fresh produce over pre-packaged items when possible.
*   **Reuse Items:** Before discarding, consider if an item can have a second life.
    *   **Food Containers:** Reuse plastic food containers from deliveries for storage.
    *   **Glass Jars:** Clean and reuse glass jars for spices or DIY projects.
    *   **Clothing Swaps/Donations:** For clothes that aren't suitable for the 의류수거함 (eui-ryu-su-geo-ham) bins but are still usable, explore local online communities (e.g., expat Facebook groups, 당근마켓 – Danggeun Market) for selling or donating.
*   **Recycle Diligently:** As detailed in this guide, make a conscious effort to separate and prepare your recyclables correctly. This ensures that the materials truly get repurposed.

### Local Initiatives and Eco-Friendly Practices

Many Korean cities and communities are actively promoting greener living:
*   **Farmers' Markets:** Support local farmers by buying fresh, often unpackaged, produce.
*   **Zero-Waste Stores:** A growing number of zero-waste or "eco-friendly" stores are popping up in major cities like Seoul. These stores allow you to bring your own containers for purchasing staples like grains, oils, and detergents, significantly reducing packaging waste. Search online for "제로 웨이스트 샵" (zero waste shop).
*   **Repair Culture:** Instead of immediately replacing broken items, consider getting them repaired. Many local shops specialize in repairing electronics, shoes, or clothing. This extends product life and reduces waste.
*   **Public Awareness Campaigns:** Keep an eye out for local government campaigns or workshops focused on sustainability and proper waste disposal. These can be great learning opportunities.
*   **Internal Linking Tip:** For those interested in exploring sustainable food options and local markets, our upcoming guide on "Eating Green: Vegetarian and Vegan Options in Seoul" will provide valuable insights.

By integrating these practices into your daily life, you not only navigate **Recycling in Korea** with ease but also actively contribute to a more sustainable future, making your expat experience richer and more meaningful.

## FAQ: Your Quick Questions Answered

### Q1: What happens if I make a mistake and put an item in the wrong recycling bin?

A1: While unintentional mistakes happen, try to minimize them. In apartment complexes, staff might sort minor errors. However, consistent or significant errors can lead to your entire bag/bin being rejected, meaning it goes to general waste. For individual houses, incorrect sorting might result in your waste being left uncollected. Repeated offenses could lead to fines. It's always best to err on the side of caution; if unsure, dispose of it in a Jongnyangje Bongtu (general waste bag) to avoid contaminating recyclables.

### Q2: Where exactly do I buy the special general waste (Jongnyangje Bongtu) and food waste (Eumsikmul Sseuregi) bags?

A2: You can purchase these special bags at almost any convenience store (e.g., GS25, CU, 7-Eleven, E-Mart 24), larger supermarkets (e.g., E-Mart, Lotte Mart, Homeplus), and even some local grocery stores. Just ask the cashier for "쓰레기 봉투" (sseuregi bongtu) for general waste or "음식물 쓰레기 봉투" (eumsikmul sseuregi bongtu) for food waste, specifying the desired size (e.g., "십 리터 주세요" – sip liteo juseyo for 10 liters).

### Q3: Can I recycle electronic waste like old phones or small appliances?

A3: Yes, but it depends on the item. Large electronics (fridges, TVs, washing machines) can be collected for free by calling 1599-0903 or scheduling online. Small electronics (old phones, hair dryers, toasters) often have designated collection points at apartment complexes, community centers (Dongsamuso), or sometimes at specific large electronics stores. Check your local Gu-cheong website for details, as rules can vary. Never put them in regular recycling or general waste due to hazardous components.

### Q4: Are there any mobile apps that can help me with recycling in Korea?

A4: While there isn't one universal, expat-friendly app for all of Korea, some districts or apartment complexes may have their own apps that provide recycling schedules and information (often in Korean). Popular apps like '여기로' (Yeogiro) are government-backed, but primarily for residents to check local waste policies and report illegal dumping. Your best bet remains checking your building's notice board, your Gu-cheong website, or asking locals. For translation of specific item names, use Papago or Google Translate.

### Q5: What should I do with old clothes or bedding that I want to get rid of?

A5: Clean, usable clothes, paired shoes, and bags can be placed in the large, green or blue "의류수거함" (eui-ryu-su-geo-ham) donation bins often found in residential areas. However, bedding (duvets, pillows, blankets), torn or dirty clothing, underwear, and carpets are generally NOT accepted in these bins. These items, if small, should be placed in a Jongnyangje Bongtu (general waste bag). Large bedding or carpets would be considered bulky waste and require a special sticker for disposal, as detailed in the "Bulky Waste" section.
