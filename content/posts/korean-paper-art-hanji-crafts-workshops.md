## The Science and Soul of Hanji

Before diving into the workshops, it is crucial to understand why this material is so revered. Hanji is created from the inner bark of the paper mulberry tree, known locally as *Dak*. While modern acidic paper yellows and crumbles within 50 to 100 years, Hanji is pH neutral (approximately 7.0 to 7.2) and can last over 1,000 years. This durability is why the "Mugujeonggwang Daedarani-gyeong," the world's oldest surviving woodblock print dating back to 751 AD, remains intact today.

## The Crafting Process: What to Expect

If you choose a Jeonji (pasting) class, which is the most popular for travelers, the process is structured to ensure success even for those with no artistic background. The glue used is typically a traditional starch paste, which is eco-friendly and odorless.

The "tearing" technique mentioned in step 2 is vital. Hanji has long fibers; when cut with scissors, the edge is sharp and visible. When torn, the fibers feather out, allowing layers to merge invisibly, creating a smooth, unified surface.

## Beyond Seoul: Jeonju, The Paper Capital

If your itinerary allows for a trip 200 kilometers south of Seoul, Jeonju is the undisputed capital of Hanji. The city produces over **70%** of the country's high-quality traditional paper. The Jeonju Hanji Museum is a must-visit destination, located about **20 minutes by taxi** from the Jeonju Hanok Village.

## DIY Kits vs. Studio Classes

For those who run out of time, many studios sell DIY kits. These kits come with pre-cut frames, paper, and glue powder. A standard kit costs between **8,000 and â‚©20,000**, which is roughly **40% cheaper** than taking the class. However, without the instructor's guidance on water-tearing and glue consistency, the results can be mixed.

<InfoBox type="warning" title="Glue Consistency Warning">
  If you buy a DIY kit to take home, be careful with the glue mixing. The starch powder must be mixed with cold water first, then heated until translucent. Using it raw will result in white, flaky residue on your artwork.
</InfoBox>
