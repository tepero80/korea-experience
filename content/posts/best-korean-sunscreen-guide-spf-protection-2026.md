## Decoding the label: What SPF 50+ and PA++++ really mean

When shopping for Korean sunscreen, you will almost exclusively see "SPF 50+ PA++++" on the packaging. While SPF (Sun Protection Factor) measures protection against UVB rays (which cause burning), the PA system is specific to Asian markets and measures protection against UVA rays (which cause aging and long-term DNA damage).

The PA system is based on the PPD (Persistent Pigment Darkening) method.
*   **PA+**: Some UVA protection.
*   **PA++**: Moderate UVA protection.
*   **PA+++**: High UVA protection.
*   **PA++++**: Extremely High UVA protection (PPD of 16 or higher).

In 2026, anything less than PA++++ is considered outdated in the Korean market. Because UVA rays penetrate glass and clouds, this high level of protection is crucial for daily wear, even when working indoors near a window.

## Ingredients to look for (and avoid)

In 2026, savvy consumers read the ingredient list before the marketing copy.

**Star Ingredients:**
*   **Centella Asiatica (Cica):** Calms redness caused by heat and sun exposure.
*   **Houttuynia Cordata (Heartleaf):** excellent for controlling acne and soothing irritation.
*   **Bamboo Water:** Provides deep hydration without oiliness.
*   **Niacinamide:** Brightens skin and controls oil production while protecting the barrier.

**Ingredients of Concern:**
*   **Oxybenzone & Octinoxate:** These chemical filters are banned in several regions (like Hawaii and Palau) due to reef damage. While effective, many Korean brands labeled "Reef Safe" or "Ocean Friendly" are phasing these out in favor of newer filters like Uvinul A Plus and Tinosorb S.
*   **Fragrance:** While pleasant, high concentrations of artificial fragrance can react with UV light and cause photosensitivity in some individuals.

## Sunscreen for medical tourism and after-care

Korea is a hub for medical tourism, specifically laser treatments and plastic surgery. After a procedure like Fraxel or Pico laser, your skin is incredibly sensitive to UV light. Post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation (PIH) is a real risk if you skip SPF.

Dermatologists will usually prescribe or recommend "clinic-grade" sunscreens available at the clinic's front desk. Brands like *Cell Fusion C* and *Histolab* are frequently used. These are specifically formulated to cover redness and protect compromised skin barriers.
