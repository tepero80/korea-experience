## The Foundation: Traditional Korean Sweets (Hangwa & Tteok)

To understand the evolution, we must start with the foundation. Traditional Korean sweets, collectively known as *Hangwa*, and rice cakes, known as *Tteok*, were historically reserved for royalty and major ceremonial events like weddings or the Chuseok harvest festival. Unlike Western desserts, which often rely on butter and heavy cream, traditional Korean sweets utilize grain syrups, honey, nuts, and pine pollen, resulting in a subtle sweetness that pairs perfectly with tea.

### The Return of Yakgwa

The undisputed star of the 2020s dessert renaissance is *Yakgwa*. These deep-fried, honey-soaked wheat cookies were once considered an "old person's snack." Today, boutique shops in Seoul sell out within 30 minutes of opening.

## The "Halmeonial" Trend: Where Old Meets New

One of the most fascinating cultural shifts is the "Halmeonial" trend (Halmoni/Grandmother + Millennial). Young Koreans are embracing traditional flavor profiles that were once considered uncool.

### Mugwort and Black Sesame

Cafes now prominently feature *Ssuk* (Mugwort) and *Heukimja* (Black Sesame) in lattes, cakes, and ice creams.
*   **Mugwort:** Offers a grassy, herbal, slightly bitter taste akin to matcha. A Mugwort Latte averages **â‚©6,500**.
*   **Black Sesame:** Offers a nutty, savory richness. Often served as a heavy cream top on iced coffee (Einspanner style).

## The Future: Health-Conscious and Vegan Adaptations

As we move through 2026, the biggest shift in the evolution of Korean desserts is the focus on health. The government has launched initiatives to reduce sugar consumption, leading to a rise in "Zero Sugar" sweets using stevia and allulose.

Furthermore, veganism, once a foreign concept, is booming. Traditional *Tteok* is naturally vegan (usually), but modern bakeries are catching up. You can now find vegan whipping cream in Bingsu and egg-free Yakgwa in major department stores like Hyundai Seoul and Shinsegae. The market for alternative desserts has grown **22% year-over-year** since 2024.
