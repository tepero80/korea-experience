## Choosing the Right Mask Material

The price often dictates the material. Understanding the difference helps you decide if the extra â‚©2,000 is worth it.

### Cotton / Microfiber (Standard)
Found in masks costing 1,000 - â‚©3,000.
*   **Pros:** Cheap, holds essence well.
*   **Cons:** Evaporates relatively quickly (20 mins max), adherence varies.

### Hydrogel (Mid-Range)
Found in masks costing 4,000 - â‚©8,000. Made of gel-like polymers.
*   **Pros:** Cooling, adheres perfectly, doesn't drip.
*   **Cons:** Can be slippery to apply, tears if handled roughly.

### Bio-Cellulose (Premium)
Found in masks costing â‚©8,000+. Made from fermented coconut water fibers.
*   **Pros:** The fibers are 1000x thinner than human hair, allowing them to reach into wrinkles and pores. Holds 100x its dry weight in fluid.
*   **Cons:** Expensive.

## Shopping Strategy: One-Day-One-Mask vs. Targeted Treatments

The "1 Day 1 Mask" trend creates a demand for bulk buying. However, dermatologists in Korea recently advised that using rich, functional masks (like whitening or anti-aging retinol masks) every day can damage the skin barrier.

**The Hybrid Strategy:**
*   **Daily:** Use cheap, simple hydration masks (Mugwort, Green Tea, Bamboo) from brands like Etude or Nature Republic (approx. â‚©1,000/day).
*   **Weekly:** Use one high-performance mask (Dr.Jart+, Sulwhasoo) for a "special treatment" (approx. â‚©10,000/week).

This strategy keeps your monthly budget around 40,000 - â‚©50,000 ($30-$37 USD) while ensuring your skin gets both maintenance and deep care.
