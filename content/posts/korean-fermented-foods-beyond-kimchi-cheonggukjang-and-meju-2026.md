## What is Meju? The Building Block of Korean Flavor

To understand any Korean fermented bean product, you must first understand Meju. Historically prepared in the late autumn (typically November), Meju consists of soybeans that have been boiled, crushed, and formed into brick-like blocks.

For more details, check out our guide on [50 Must Try Korean Foods Complete Guide](/blog/50-must-try-korean-foods-complete-guide-2026).

These blocks are then hung to dry and ferment naturally. The process relies on *Bacillus subtilis* and various fungi found naturally in the air, particularly in the Korean countryside.

The transformation of Meju is fascinating. A typical brick measures approximately **20cm x 15cm x 10cm**. During the drying phase, the outer layer hardens while the inside retains moisture, allowing beneficial molds to grow. By early 2026, artisanal producers in regions like Sunchang and Paju are selling premium Meju bricks for approximately **45,000 to 60,000 KRW** per block, a 15% increase from 2024 prices due to rising domestic soybean costs.

For more details, check out our guide on [Korean Breakfast Foods Traditional Morning Meals Guide](/blog/korean-breakfast-foods-traditional-morning-meals).

<InfoBox type="info" title="The Smell of Tradition">
  If you visit rural Korea in winter, you might see Meju blocks hanging from eaves. They emit a unique, musty odor. This is not spoilage; it is the smell of beneficial bacteria working to break down proteins into amino acids, creating the savory taste known as Umami.
</InfoBox>

Once fully fermented, these bricks are placed in brine to produce two staples simultaneously: the liquid becomes Ganjang (soup soy sauce), and the remaining solids become Doenjang (soybean paste).

## Cheonggukjang: The Fast-Fermented Powerhouse

While Doenjang takes months or years to mature, Cheonggukjang is the sprinter of the fermented bean world. It is made by fermenting boiled soybeans in a warm place (traditionally the *ondol* heated floor) for just **2 to 3 days** (48-72 hours).

The result is a sticky, stringy paste that bears a striking resemblance to Japanese Natto, though the bacterial strains differ slightly. The defining characteristic of Cheonggukjang is its intense smellâ€”often compared to wet socks or strong ripe cheeseâ€”and its incredible depth of flavor.

## Cooking Tips for the Brave: Managing the Odor

If you decide to cook Cheonggukjang stew in your apartment or home, you need an odor management strategy. The smell can permeate fabrics and linger for **24 to 48 hours** if not managed.

<InfoBox type="tip" title="Odor Neutralization">
  Korean grandmothers swear by lighting a candle during and after cooking Cheonggukjang. The flame burns off some of the odorous compounds in the air. Alternatively, boiling vinegar and water in a separate pot for 15 minutes after cooking can neutralize the alkaline-heavy smell of the beans.
</InfoBox>

When cooking, the ratio is crucial. For a standard pot (serves 2-3), use roughly **150g** of Cheonggukjang paste. Unlike Doenjang, which is added early to develop depth, Cheonggukjang should be added towards the *end* of the cooking process (last 5-10 minutes) to preserve the beneficial bacteria (probiotics), which are heat-sensitive.
