Head to the Google Play Store or Apple App Store and search for "Emergency Ready App." Itâ€™s the one with the blue icon developed by MOIS (Ministry of the Interior and Safety). Once opened, accept the permissionsâ€”yes, it needs your location, don't be shy.

On the first launch, it should detect your system language, but if it boots up in Korean, look for the gear icon (Settings/ì„¤ì •) usually at the bottom or top right. Select "Language" and choose English. The interface will reload.

## FAQ

### Q1: Why does my phone make a terrifying loud noise at random times?
That is the **CBS (Cell Broadcasting Service)**. Korea uses this aggressively for everything from missing elderly people (ambiguous "safety alerts") to actual air raid drills. The loud screeching is usually for "Emergency" level events, while a simple buzz is for "Safety" alerts (like weather or missing persons).

### Q2: Can I turn off the Korean alerts and just use the English app?
Technically, yes, on your phone's main OS settings under "Wireless Emergency Alerts," you can toggle off "Public Safety Alerts." However, I **strongly advise against this**. The Korean alert is the fastest. Keep it on, endure the noise, and use the English app to translate it.

### Q3: What is a "Civil Defense Drill" and why did everything stop?
As of 2026, Korea conducts regular Civil Defense Drills (usually in May/August). Sirens wail for about 15-20 minutes. Cars must pull over, and pedestrians might be directed into subway stations. **Do not panic.** It is just a drill. The Emergency Ready App will usually send a notification *before* the drill starts to warn you it's a test.

### Q4: Does the app work offline?
Partially. The directory of shelters and medical centers is often downloaded to your phone, so you can find a hospital on the map without data. However, receiving *new* alerts requires an active internet connection (WiFi or Data).

### Q5: The app says "Missile," should I run?
Okay, deep breath. In the rare event of a "Wartime Alert" or "Air Raid," the alert sound is distinct (a wavy, rising-falling siren). The text will be Red. The app will direct you to the nearest "Underground Shelter" (usually a subway station or apartment basement). If you see this, follow the locals. If they are running, you run. If they are buying coffee, it might be a mistranslation or a drill.

### Section 2: The Background Story

So, how did we get here? Why is Korea so intense about these alerts?

To understand the app, you have to understand the Korean mindset of *Ppalli Ppalli* (Hurry, Hurry) applied to safety. South Korea has a incredibly robust telecommunications infrastructure. They realized years ago that they could reach 99% of the population instantly through Cell Broadcasting Service (CBS).

Back in the day (we're talking pre-2014), foreigners were largely left out of this loop. If a typhoon was hitting Busan, expats would just look out the window and guess. But after several high-profile safety incidents and a massive push to make Korea more tourist-friendly for events like the PyeongChang Olympics, the Ministry of the Interior and Safety (MOIS) realized they had a gap. They had millions of tourists and residents who couldn't understand "Danger! Evacuate!"

Thus, the Emergency Ready App was born. Initially, it was clunky. Honestly, it was pretty bad. But fast forward to 2024 and 2025, and the government poured resources into it. They expanded the languages to include Thai and Vietnamese because of the changing demographics of foreign residents in Korea. They integrated it with Civil Defense Drills (which resumed recently due to geopolitical tensions).

By 2026, this app has become a staple of the "Living in Korea" starter pack. It's not just an app anymore; it's a lifeline that connects the non-Korean speaker to the massive, pulsating nervous system of Korean civil safety. It bridges the gap between hearing a siren and *knowing* you're safe.
