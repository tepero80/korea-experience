## FAQ

### Q1: Can I buy antibiotics over the counter in Korea?
No, absolutely not. Korea has very strict antibiotic regulations to prevent resistance. You must see a doctor at a clinic (approx. ‚Ç©20,000 for a visit without insurance) to get a prescription.

### Q2: What is the number for a medical emergency if I don't speak Korean?
Dial **119** for an ambulance. They have interpretation services. If it's not life-threatening but you need medical advice, dial **1339** (Korea Disease Control) or **1330** (Travel Hotline) for English assistance.

### Q3: Do Korean pharmacists speak English?
In major districts like Gangnam, Hongdae, and Itaewon, yes, usually enough to help. In smaller neighborhoods, it's hit or miss. Use Papago (translation app) or show a picture of the medicine you use back home‚Äîthey can usually match the active ingredients.

### Q4: Is Tylenol available at convenience stores?
Yes. You can find Tylenol (Acetaminophen), Brufen (Ibuprofen) syrup for kids, and basic digestive drinks at CU, GS25, and 7-Eleven 24/7. Look for the small medical shelf near the cashier.

### Q5: Why do pharmacies close so early in Korea?
Most clinics close around 6 PM or 7 PM, and since pharmacies rely heavily on filling prescriptions from nearby clinics, they close when the doctors go home. The "Public Night Pharmacy" system is a government initiative to fix this gap.
