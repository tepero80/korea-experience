## FAQ

### Q1: Is it safe to get plastic surgery in Korea in 2026?
Absolutely, probably safer than ever. The mandatory CCTV law passed in 2023 is fully effective now, meaning clinics must record surgeries if you ask. This has virtually eliminated the "ghost doctor" (unqualified substitutes) issue that plagued the industry years ago.

### Q2: How much cheaper is it really compared to the US?
It's significant. A complex revision rhinoplasty that might cost $20,000+ in Beverly Hills will likely run you $8,000-$12,000 in Seoul. For primary rhinoplasty, you're looking at $4,000 vs. $12,000. Plus, the price often includes aftercare, meds, and sometimes even airport transfers.

### Q3: Do I really need to stay for 14 days?
Ideally, yes. Stitches and casts usually come off on Day 7. However, flying immediately after isn't great due to cabin pressure affecting swelling. Plus, if you have any minor infection or issue, you want to be there to fix it. If you *must* leave, Day 8 is the absolute earliest, but get your surgeon's clearance.

### Q4: What is "Rib Rhinoplasty" and why do Korean doctors love it?
Korean surgeons are masters of using autologous rib cartilage (from your own body) or donated rib. They prefer it for building up the bridge or tip because it's strong, ample, and doesn't reject like synthetic implants can. It's especially common for revision cases or for people who need significant structure added to a smaller nose.

### Q5: Will I be able to breathe properly afterwards?
Functional rhinoplasty is huge in Korea now. Most top clinics will do a CT scan to check for deviated septums or turbinate hypertrophy. They often fix these breathing issues *during* the cosmetic surgery. So yes, many patients actually breathe *better* after recovery.
