## PROS AND CONS

### Pros (of the eSIM - Recommended)
1.  **Freedom from "The Swap"**: Look, nobody likes fumbling for a paperclip on an airplane tray table to pop out a tiny SIM card. With an eSIM, you keep your home number active (great for receiving bank OTPs) while using Korean data.
2.  **Instant Gratification**: You can buy it while sitting in your living room. By the time the plane wheels touch the tarmac at Incheon, you just toggle it on, and boom‚Äîyou're live. No waiting in line at the counter while jet-lagged.
3.  **Price Performance**: Without the cost of the plastic chip and the store overhead, eSIM plans in Korea have become dirt cheap. You're often paying less for unlimited data than you would for a single fancy coffee in Gangnam.

### Cons (be honest!)
1.  **The "Verification" Wall**: Most tourist eSIMs are "Data Only." Even if you buy one with a number, you **cannot** use it for "Identity Verification" (online banking, signing up for local websites) because you don't have a Resident Card (ARC). It's a number, but it's not a "verified" identity.
2.  **Battery Drain**: While better than searching for a signal constantly, running 5G data all day on your primary phone will zap your battery faster than usual. You'll need a power bank (which, let's be honest, you need in Korea anyway).
3.  **Compatibility Roulette**: If you have a phone older than an iPhone XR or a Galaxy S20, you might be out of luck. Also, if you forgot to unlock your phone with your carrier back home, the eSIM simply won't install.

## STEP-BY-STEP GUIDE

**Guide Title:** How to Go from "Offline" to "Connected" in 2026 Korea
**Total Time:** 15 Minutes (eSIM) / 30 Minutes (Physical)
**Difficulty:** Easy

### Step 1: The Pre-Flight Check (Crucial!)
Before you even leave your house, call your home carrier. Ask them specifically: "Is my device unlocked for international use?" If they say no, get them to unlock it.
> **Pro Tip:** While you're at it, download **Naver Map** and **KakaoTalk**. Google Maps is notoriously bad in Korea (it doesn't show walking directions well), and you'll need these apps immediately.

### Step 2: Purchase Your Plan
Buy your eSIM online via platforms like **Klook**, **Trazy**, or directly from carrier sites (SKT Roaming, KT Roaming). Do this about 1-2 days before you fly. You'll receive a QR code via email.
> **Pro Tip:** Take a screenshot of the QR code and send it to your travel partner, or print it out. You can't scan a QR code that's *on* the screen of the phone you're trying to set up!

### Step 3: The Airport Hustle (If buying physical/picking up)
If you chose a physical SIM or Wi-Fi Egg, head to the "Roaming Center" in the Arrivals Hall (1F) of Incheon Airport. Look for SKT, KT, or LG U+. In 2026, be prepared for a quick **facial scan** at the counter‚Äîit's the new law to prevent fraud.
> **Pro Tip:** The queues at the "Roaming Centers" near the central exits (Exit 7-8) are always huge. Walk 50 meters to the counters near the far ends (Exit 3 or Exit 12); they are usually empty.

### Step 4: Activation and "The Restart"
For eSIM: Go to Settings > Cellular > Add eSIM > Scan QR. Label your new plan "Korea." **Turn off** "Data Roaming" for your home SIM and **Turn on** "Data Roaming" for the Korea eSIM.
> **Pro Tip:** Your internet might not work instantly. Toggle "Airplane Mode" on and off three times. This forces the phone to ping the nearest Korean cell tower (usually KT or SKT) and register your device.

## FAQ

### Q1: Can I use my home SIM and a Korean eSIM at the same time?
Yes! This is the magic of dual SIM. You can keep your home number active (to receive texts from your bank or calls from family) while setting your phone to use the Korean eSIM for all data. Just make sure "Data Roaming" is OFF for your home SIM so you don't get a massive bill.

### Q2: Is 5G really necessary, or is 4G LTE enough?
Honestly, 4G LTE in Korea is faster than 5G in most other countries. You likely won't notice the difference for maps, Instagram, or browsing. However, since the price difference is usually only $2-$3, I say treat yourself to the 5G speeds‚Äîit makes loading image-heavy reviews on Naver Map instant.

### Q3: I'm traveling with my family (4 people). Should we get 4 eSIMs?
If you stick together 24/7, get a **Wi-Fi Egg**. It allows up to 3-5 connections and is much cheaper ($3/day total vs $15/person). However, if you plan to split up (e.g., one person goes shopping, one goes to a cafe), you *must* have individual eSIMs, or you will lose contact.

### Q4: Do I need a VPN in Korea?
Korea does filter some internet content (mostly adult sites and some gambling sites), but for general travel needs (Netflix, YouTube, Social Media), everything works fine. You generally don't *need* a VPN for speed or access, but it's always good for security on public hotel Wi-Fi.

### Q5: What is this "Facial Recognition" requirement I keep hearing about?
Starting in March 2026, the Korean government mandates that all new SIM activations (even for tourists) require a facial scan to match your passport photo. This is to stop voice phishing scams. It takes 30 seconds at the counter or via the app, so don't be alarmed‚Äîit's just a new security step.

### Section 2: The Background Story
Korea has always been a bit of a "future world" when it comes to the internet. Back in 2010, while the rest of the world was happy with 3G, Seoul was already blanketing its subways with lightning-fast Wi-Fi. For the longest time, the "Wi-Fi Egg" (a portable router) was the symbol of the Korean tourist. You‚Äôd rent it at Incheon, carry it in your pocket, and pray the battery didn't die while you were navigating the maze of Gangnam Station.

But the game changed around 2022 when eSIM technology finally went mainstream in Korea. The "Big 3" carriers‚ÄîSK Telecom (SKT), KT, and LG U+‚Äîrealized that tourists didn't want to swap plastic chips anymore. They started offering competitive digital plans.

Fast forward to 2026, and things have gotten serious‚Äîliterally. The Korean government, dealing with a rise in voice phishing scams, has implemented strict new regulations. Starting March 2026, activating a SIM card (even a tourist one) isn't just about showing a passport; it now requires **facial recognition verification** at the point of sale to match your ID. This sounds scary, but it's really just a 30-second scan. It does mean, however, that the days of buying a "burner SIM" from a shady stall are over. Everything is tracked, secure, and highly regulated.

If you run out of data, hop on a subway or bus. Korea's public transit Wi-Fi is incredibly fast. However, it's often secure.
