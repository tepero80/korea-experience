## PROS AND CONS

### Pros (of following Korean Drinking Etiquette)
1. **Instant Respect**: Honestly, nothing impresses a Korean host more than a foreigner who knows to turn their head when drinking with an elder. It instantly bridges the cultural gap and shows you care.

## STEP-BY-STEP GUIDE

**Guide Title:** The Art of the Perfect Pour & Receive
**Total Time:** 1 Minute (Repeated all night)
**Difficulty:** Medium (Requires coordination and social awareness)

### Step 1: The Setup (Reading the Room)
Before a drop is spilled, figure out the age hierarchy. If you don't know ages, assume anyone who looks senior to you *is* senior.
> **Pro Tip:** If you're unsure, just use the most formal etiquette. It's better to be too polite than rude. If they want you to relax, they will tell you, "Ay, put your hands down!" (meaning you can use one hand).

### Step 2: Receiving the Drink
When someone picks up the bottle to pour for you, **stop what you are doing**. Pick up your glass. Hold the glass with your right hand. Place your left hand palm-down under your right elbow (or wrist). This "support" arm is a vestige of holding back long sleeves in the old days, but now it means respect.
> **Pro Tip:** Lift your glass slightly off the table to meet the bottle, but not too high‚Äîdon't make them reach up to you.

### Step 3: The Toast and The Turn
After "Gunbae!" (Cheers), check who you are with. If you are drinking with an elder (senior, boss, older relative), you **must** turn your upper body and head *away* from them. Cover the mouth of the glass with your left hand and take the sip discreetly. You are essentially hiding the act of consuming alcohol from them out of modesty.
> **Pro Tip:** If you are sitting across from them, turn 90 degrees to the side. If you are next to them, turn roughly 45 degrees away.

### Step 4: The Return Pour
Now it's your turn. Never let an elder's glass sit empty. Pick up the bottle with your right hand. Support your right arm with your left hand (under the elbow or wrist). **Cover the label** of the bottle with your hand (a superstition/politeness habit). Pour until the glass is about 80-90% full.
> **Pro Tip:** Never fill your own glass. If yours is empty, wait. Someone will notice. If they don't, you can subtly touch the bottle to signal you're ready, but usually, just pouring for someone else triggers them to pour for you.

## FAQ

### Q1: Do I really have to turn my head away when I drink?
Yes, if the person is older or your boss. It feels awkward at first, like you're snubbing them, but looking them in the eye while taking a shot is considered challenging or disrespectful. If you are friends/peers, you can look at each other.

### Q2: What is "Somaek"?
Somaek (ÏÜåÎß•) is a portmanteau of **So**ju and **Maek**ju (beer). It‚Äôs a boilermaker. You drop a shot of soju into a glass of beer. It is delicious, dangerous, and the quickest way to get a party started. The "Golden Ratio" is usually 3 parts soju to 7 parts beer, but beware‚Äîit hits harder than you think.

### Q3: How do I say "Cheers" in Korean?
The most common is "Gunbae!" (Í±¥Î∞∞). Literally "dry glass." If you want to be cool and you're with a younger crowd, you might hear "Jjan!" (Ïß†) which represents the sound of glasses clinking. Use "Gunbae" for formal, "Jjan" for friends.

### Q4: Can I refuse a drink if I don't drink alcohol?
This is tricky. In 2026, things are better than they used to be. You can say you are on medication or have a health issue. However, you should still *accept* the glass. Let them pour it for you. Then, just let it sit there. You can touch it to your lips during a toast, but put it down full. Alternatively, swap your shot glass for water and toast with that‚Äîintent matters more than the liquid.

### Q5: Who pays the bill?
In Korean culture, the person who invited you (or the senior/oldest person) almost always pays. Splitting the bill (Dutch Pay) is becoming more common among young friends (Gen Z), but in a business or formal setting, one person pays for the whole round. You can offer to pay for "Cha" (round 2) at a coffee shop or bar.

### [Section 2: The Background Story]

You might be wondering, "Why is this so complicated? Can't we just drink?"

Well, blame the ancestors. This whole system is rooted in Confucianism, which is basically the operating system of Korean society. Confucianism loves hierarchy‚ÄîElder vs. Younger, Senior vs. Junior, Boss vs. Employee. Alcohol was historically considered a "sacred" drink, used in rites to honor ancestors, so the etiquette around it had to be formal.

Back in the day, you would hide your drinking from elders because showing your intoxicated face or teeth was considered rude. That‚Äôs where the "turn away" move comes from.

Fast forward to the modern era. The "Green Bottle" soju we know today really took over in the 1970s when the government banned using rice for alcohol due to food shortages. Makers switched to sweet potatoes and tapioca, creating a cheap, high-alcohol spirit. It fueled the rapid industrialization of Korea. Workers would finish a 14-hour shift and bond over cheap soju. The etiquette kept the social fabric tight even when everyone was falling-over drunk.

By 2026, things have shifted. We have "Zero Sugar" soju (thanks, Saero!) because everyone is health-conscious now. We have "Highballs" taking over convenience stores. But that ancient respect? It hasn't gone anywhere.

### [Section 4: The Complete How-To]

Okay, let‚Äôs get practical. You are at the table. The bottle is in front of you.

**Step 1: The Pour**

*   *Pro Tip:* If you want to be extra pro, cover the label of the bottle with your hand so it doesn‚Äôt show. This is an old-school move that shows humility.

**Step 2: Receiving**

**Step 3: The Drink**

**Step 4: The Refill**

*   *Exception:* With beer, you can top off a half-full glass. But soju is strictly "empty-to-full."
