## STEP-BY-STEP GUIDE

**Guide Title:** How to Book and Survive Your First Temple Stay
**Total Time:** 30 minutes (booking) + 2 Days (experience)
**Difficulty:** Medium (Booking can be finicky; Experience requires patience)

### Step 1: Choosing Your Temple
Don't just pick a random one. Do you want to hike? Pick a mountain temple like **Seoraksan**. Do you want food? Pick **Jinkwansa** or **Baekyangsa**. Do you want to stay in the city? Pick **Bongeunsa** (Gangnam) or **Jogyesa** (Insadong).
> **Pro Tip:** If it's your first time and you don't speak Korean, filter for temples that explicitly state "English Program Available" on the Templestay website.

### Step 2: The Booking Process
Go to the official website (eng.templestay.com). It‚Äôs the only reliable place. Select your region and date. You will usually need to pay via credit card immediately to secure the spot.
> **Pro Tip:** The English website can be glitchy with foreign credit cards. If it fails, try using the desktop version rather than mobile, or look for the temple's direct email in the "Contact" section to ask for a PayPal invoice or bank transfer option.

### Step 3: Packing the Essentials
You don't need a sleeping bag, but you DO need toiletries. Temples provide soap, but rarely shampoo or toothpaste. Bring a water bottle, a towel (they are often small), and slip-on shoes.
> **Pro Tip:** **BRING SOCKS.** I cannot stress this enough. You will take your shoes off and put them on 50 times a day. Being barefoot in a Buddha hall is considered rude (and in winter, the floors are cold). Wear clean, hole-free socks.

### Step 4: Arrival and Etiquette
Check-in is usually around 2:00 PM or 3:00 PM. You‚Äôll be given a uniform (a vest and baggy pants). Change immediately. Leave your ego and your loud voice at the gate.
> **Pro Tip:** When you see a monk or nun, you don't need to say "Hello." Just stop, put your hands together in prayer position (Hapjang), and do a half-bow. It‚Äôs the universal sign of respect there.

## WARNINGS AND TIPS

### üí° Pro Tip
**Winter is Cold, Summer is Hot.** Korean temples are often old wooden buildings. While the floors are heated (Ondol), the air can be freezing in January. Bring thermal layers to wear *under* your temple uniform. In summer? Bug spray. Mountain mosquitoes are no joke.

### ‚ö†Ô∏è Warning
**Don't Waste Food.** In the temple, taking food and not eating it is bad karma and bad manners. "Baru Gongyang" (formal meal) requires you to clean your bowl with a slice of radish and water and drink the water. Take small portions; you can always go back for seconds.

### üì± No Korean Phone/ARC? Here's What To Do
This is a massive pain point for tourists. The online payment systems often demand a Korean phone verification or "3D Secure" that fails on foreign cards.
**The Solution:**
1.  **Email the temple directly.** Find the "Program Manager" email on the specific temple's page.
2.  Subject line: "Foreigner Reservation Inquiry - [Date] - [Name]"
3.  Write: "I do not have a Korean phone number or ARC. Can I pay via PayPal or pay cash upon arrival?"
4.  Many temples (especially popular ones like Golgulsa or Haeinsa) are used to this and will manually reserve your spot.

## FAQ

### Q1: Do I have to be Buddhist to participate?
Absolutely not. The program was literally invented to host soccer fans during the World Cup. It is designed as a cultural experience, not a religious conversion camp. You won't be forced to convert, though you are expected to respect the rituals.

### Q2: Is the food vegan?
Yes, almost exclusively. Temple food prohibits meat, fish, and the "five pungent spices" (garlic, green onions, chives, onions, leeks) because they are believed to distract the mind during meditation. It is vegan-friendly and incredibly delicious.

### Q3: Can I share a room with my partner/spouse?
It depends on the temple. Traditionally, men and women sleep in separate quarters. However, many "tourist-friendly" temples now offer private rooms for couples or families. You **must** check this before booking; otherwise, you might be waving goodnight to your spouse across the courtyard.

### Q4: Is there Wi-Fi?
Usually, yes, but only in the main office area or near the cafeteria. The sleeping quarters often don't have it. But seriously, try to use your data sparingly. You're there to disconnect!

### Q5: What is "108 Bows"? Do I have to do it?
It is a physical meditation where you bow down to the floor 108 times, representing the clearing of 108 anxieties/delusions. It takes about 20-30 minutes and is a workout! In "Experience" programs, it's encouraged; in "Rest" programs, it's optional. If you have bad knees, you can just sit and meditate instead.

### Section 2: The Background Story

Okay, so how did this become a "thing"? It‚Äôs actually a pretty cool story. Go back to **2002**. South Korea and Japan were co-hosting the **FIFA World Cup**. It was a massive event, and Korea had a problem: they didn't have enough hotel rooms for the flood of international soccer fans coming in.

The government and the **Jogye Order** (the biggest sect of Buddhism in Korea) had a lightbulb moment. They thought, "We have all these beautiful, historic temples in the mountains with plenty of floor space. Why don't we let the tourists sleep there?".

It started as a practical solution to a housing shortage, but something unexpected happened. The soccer fans *loved* it. It wasn't just a place to crash; it was a total cultural immersion. The government realized they had struck gold. They formalized it, created the **Cultural Corps of Korean Buddhism** in 2004, and turned it into a permanent tourism staple. Suddenly, foodies from New York and Paris were trekking to Korean mountains not just for enlightenment, but for the kimchi. **Booking:** You absolutely need to book in advance. Go to **eng.templestay.com**. It‚Äôs the official English portal. You can search by region or by activity (like "Martial Arts" or "Tea Ceremony").
